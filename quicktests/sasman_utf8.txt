The compressible turbulent boundary layer is expressed in terms of the momentum integral
and moment-of-momentum integral equations for arbitrary pressure gradient. Upon simpli-
fication of this equation set with a Mager-type transformation, the two coupled differential
equations are solved simultaneously to provide boundary-layer momentum thickness, dis-
placement thickness, skin-friction coefficient, and related quantities as a function of the body
surface coordinate. The analysis includes surface heat-transfer effects. Results of the
analysis are presented for adiabatic, non-adiabatic, two-dimensional, and axisymmetric flow-
fields in a Mach number range between 2 and 8. Flows with favorable and adverse pressure
gradients are also examined. The present analysis is compared with available experimental
and theoretical results. The analysis is found to yield satisfactory results for flows with and
without heat transfer. In addition, for flows in the pressure of large adverse pressure gradients
in which the boundary layer is known to be well-behaved, spurious separation is not indi-
cated; instead, rather good agreement with available experimental data is observed.

I. Introduction
THE turbulent boundary layer has long been the subject
of investigation in fluid mechanics. In recent years the
understanding of detailed phenomena relating thereto has
been advanced significantly with concepts such as the law of
the wall and the law of the wake. However, despite these
new techniques, a general treatment of the compressible tur-
bulent boundary layer employing these methods has yet to be
accomplished.
From a pragmatic point of view, it is highly desirable to
couch the solution to the forementioned problem only in
terms of known variables of body geometry and the associated
inviscid flowfield conditions, rather than to appeal to pa-
rameters, such as the velocity defect, which have a funda-
mental importance in turbulent boundary-layer analysis.
Indeed, many investigators, motivated on grounds of prac-
ticality and attracted by the apparent conceptual simplicity,
have expressed their analyses and results of turbulent bound-
ary-layer behavior in terms of the boundary-layer form factor
(H) and momentum thickness (8). It should be observed,
however, that the boundary-layer form factor can be regarded
as something less than an ideal parameter for turbulent
boundary-layer analysis, since it is generally an unestablished
function of pressure distribution and skin friction.1 None-
theless, withstanding such apparent shortcomings, in the
past a significant amount of work has been accomplished with
the aid of the form factor parameter. For incompressible
flows, for example, Von Doenhoff and Tetervin2 used the

momentum equation together with an empirical auxiliary
equation to calculate boundary-layer form factor distribution.
More recently, Tetervin and Lin3 systematically expressed
the boundary-layer momentum, moment-of-momentum, and
energy integral equations using the form factor concept and
applied these results with reasonable success in the prediction
of turbulent separation on subsonic airfoils. Maskell4 has
also extended this process of turbulent boundary-layer analysis
by describing the variation of H with x by a series of empirical
functions that conveniently uncouple the variation of H from
the momentum integral equation.
For turbulent boundary layers in compressible flow,
Reshotko and Tucker5 again used the momentum and
moment-of-momentum integral equations in terms of the form
factor and the momentum thickness. The Stewartson trans-
formation was used together with the results of Maskell to
express the equations in an incompressible form and to un-
couple the two governing equations. This analysis resulted
in equations that should tentatively describe turbulent bound-
ary layers in compressible flow with heat transfer. The
Reshotko-Tucker analysis, which has enjoyed success for
favorable and mild adverse pressure gradient, however,
possesses some shortcomings when applied to flows with large
adverse gradients. One of the more serious of these is an in-
dicated boundary-layer separation in regions where the actual
boundary layer is reasonably well-behaved. In particular, the
unsatisfactory behavior of the analysis, when applied to a
flat plate followed by a curved compression surface, has been
demonstrated by McLafferty and Barber.6 Reference 6 has
also presented an empirical method of analysis which pro-
vides reasonable agreement with their experimental results.
The analysis presented herein attempts to eliminate some
shortcomings of the earlier methods by incorporating more
recently available results of turbulent boundary-layer
analyses. This analysis is similar, in some respects, to the
work of Ref. 5; the boundary-layer momentum integral
equation is used in conjunction with the moment-of-momen-
tum equation. The wall shearing stress is given by the Lud-
wieg-Tillmann7 formulation. However, unlike previous in-
vestigations no attempt is made to uncouple the momentum
and moment-of-momentum integral equations. Instead, the
set of two differential equations is solved simultaneously after
introduction of normalized boundary-layer shearing stress
distributions. The shear distributions are established from
numerical results of equilibrium turbulent boundary-layer
analysis.

D. Flows with Favorable Pressure Gradient
Several favorable and favorable-adverse pressure gradient
flows were also considered. The first case in point is repre-
sented by data from two California Institute of Technology
Jet Propulsion Laboratory supersonic nozzles, as shown in
Fig. 10. Calculations were initiated at the nozzle throats
using initial 0 values listed in Ref. 20. It may be seen that
both <5* and 6 agree closely with the experimental points ob-
tained by Dayman20 for the Mach 5 and the Mach 2 adiabatic
nozzles.
Secondly, Mach 2 flow past an axisymmetric waisted body
of Ref. 21 is examined (Fig. 11). Calculation was initiated in
the boundary-layer transition region. Again, it may be ob-
served that 6 is in good agreement with data points, as is
Cf/2, which falls within the range of experimental error stated
in Ref. 21. Also shown are the analysis curves of this ref-
erence. The analysis curves were obtained by approximating
the very difficult task of simultaneous integration of both
momentum and energy integral equations through preliminary
correlations of the adiabatic data to be analyzed subsequently.
Such an analysis, in which direct consideration is given to the
energy equation, might indeed be promising if the initial dif-
ficulties of this approach can be transcended. As shown in
Ref. 21, extension of such an approach to other than adiabatic
flows within a small Mach number range appears to be
reasonably difficult.
IV. Conclusions
The results of the present analysis indicate that the simul-
taneous solution of the boundary-layer integral equations,
without attempting to uncouple the same, may indeed be a
possible method for treatment of the general compressible
turbulent boundary layer. The present method yields skin-
friction coefficients and other boundary-layer parameters in
close agreement with experimental and theoretical results over
a wide range of zero, favorable, and adverse pressure-gradient
flows. Favorable results for nonadiabatic flows with zero
and mild pressure gradients are also obtained.
In addition, it was found that this method of analysis did
not give spurious indication of separation in the presence of
large adverse pressure gradients, but yielded boundary-layer
parameters in good agreement with experimentally obtained
data.
